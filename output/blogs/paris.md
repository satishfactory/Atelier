# Paris — Mont-marte, 2019

This painting carries more weight than it shows. On the surface it is a tabletop with a bottle and glass, a view of Parisian architecture, a shuttered facade — still life meeting city memory. But underneath it holds two deaths and a refusal to let grief have the last word. Dawn left us suddenly. My father left this world while I was in Colmar. The painting does not illustrate either of those losses. It holds them in its colour, in its warmth, in the fact that it exists at all.

The source material was old postcards I bought in the hidden lanes of Paris during a visit in 2018. Not the tourist Paris — the other one, the one you find when you turn the wrong way and end up in a lane where someone is selling paper from another century. Those postcards had a particular quality of light already built into them, already faded, already belonging to a time that had passed. That is what I wanted — not Paris as it is, but Paris as it remains in the hand after you leave.

I built the painting from interlocking planes rather than observation. The diagonal of the tabletop pushes the eye into the receding street-wall, and the shutter at the upper right stops the movement before it escapes. The composition is compartmentalised — each zone has its own logic, its own colour temperature, its own job. Some of the collage papers are from India, which means the surface itself is carrying geography. Apricot and gold against lavender and blue-grey. The colour is doing structural work, not describing light. It is making a space that feels sun-filled and contemplative without pointing at any specific hour or season.

The influences are clear enough to me — the southern light, domestic objects given the same weight as architecture, decorative pattern treated as seriously as form. Matisse is in the room, obviously. But so is the experience of sitting in a café in Montmartre knowing that the person across from you will not be there next year, and that your father is dying in another country, and that the wine in the glass is still warm.

The painting sold, which means it lives somewhere else now. I am glad it found a home. What stays with me is the palette — those apricots and golds carry something I have not fully exhausted. The warmth in this painting is not decorative. It is an argument. It says that colour can hold loss without performing it, that a sunlit surface is not the same as denial. Whether I fully succeeded in that argument, I am still not sure. But the painting does not lie. It holds what it holds. Dawn is in it. My father is in it. Paris is in it. The colours say what I could not say out loud at the time — that life continues, that light returns, that this is not the same as forgetting.
