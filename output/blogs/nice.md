# Nice

This painting carries a weight it never announces. I made it in 2019, after a trip to the South of France — Colmar and Nice — and on the surface it is exactly what it looks like: shutters, a vase, fruit on a table, floral patterns, the geometry of a domestic interior seen through the filter of Mediterranean light. But the trip was interrupted. I received the news of my father's passing while I was there, and the painting became something I had not planned. It became the last thing I was looking at before everything changed.

I worked from travel photographs, but I was not interested in describing a place. What stayed with me were fragments — the repeating rhythm of shutters, the way flowers appeared on balconies and tablecloths and tiles as if the same motif were migrating through every surface of the town. I built the painting as a grid of compartments, each one holding a piece of that visual memory. A window here, a floral pattern there, a tabletop arrangement in another zone. The structure is architectonic — almost like looking at a building's façade flattened into a quilt — but the colour keeps it intimate. Soft yellows, mauves, dusty blues, rose accents. Nothing dramatic. Everything at the temperature of a room you have been sitting in quietly for a long time.

The key decision was to let the compartments rhyme with each other rather than compete. Each zone echoes the shapes and lines of the others, so the eye moves fluidly across a surface that could easily have felt rigid or decorative in a shallow way. I wanted the patterning to do the emotional work — not to illustrate grief, which was not something I could have painted directly at that time, but to create a sense of shelter. Of being held inside a space that is ordered and gentle and does not ask anything of you. The painting is quiet in the way a room is quiet after everyone has left.

I did not understand this painting when I finished it. I thought it was a decorative exercise, a record of a trip. It took time to see that the compartmentalised structure was doing something psychological — holding things in separate containers because I could not yet let them touch. The domestic imagery was not nostalgia. It was the last intact world before the loss, preserved in segments like specimens. The calm surface is real, but it is the calm of someone who has not yet begun to grieve.

What remains unresolved is whether the painting knows this about itself or whether I am reading it backwards. I think the honest answer is both. The painting was smarter than I was in 2019. It organised the material I could not yet face into a structure that would hold it until I was ready. I am only now beginning to understand what it was doing.
