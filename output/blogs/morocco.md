# Morocco

This painting is a place I have never been. That matters. It is not a travel painting — it is a longing painting. Morocco exists for me entirely through pictures, through Matisse's time there, through Van Gogh's yellow house in Arles which is not Morocco at all but carries the same weight of southern light hitting flat walls. I made this painting in 2019 from that accumulated wanting, and it remains one of the most deliberate compositions I have produced. A smaller version sold, and I was glad, but this is the one I kept at 36×36 because this is the size where the street actually opens up and lets you walk into it.

The composition splits cleanly into two theatres. On the left, a diagonal recession pulls you down a street or alley — observed architecture, perspective lines, the kind of spatial depth that comes from looking at photographs of Moroccan towns until the geometry lives in your hand. On the right, everything stacks vertically: the mannequin-like figure, the vessels, the black gridded shape, the ornamental violet field. That right side is not a street. It is an altar. The black mass running through the centre — a road, a shadow, a division — turns the whole picture into a stage. I wanted that split. The left side says: this is a place. The right side says: this is what the place means to me.

The references are specific and I am not hiding them. Matisse's Moroccan fruit vendor gave me the figure with the vessels. Van Gogh's Yellow House gave me the courage to let architectural colour be bold and flat and not apologise for it. The pastel tones of the buildings sit against that emphatic violet and black and red, and I remember making the decision to push the violet harder than felt comfortable. The painting needed it. Without the violet, the right side collapses into decoration. With it, the whole surface becomes graphic and slightly ceremonial — as if the city has been translated into a private alphabet.

What I discovered making this painting is that you can reconstruct a place you have never visited and arrive at something more honest than a travel sketch. The reconstruction strips away the accidental. What remains is the essential architecture of fascination — why Morocco pulls at me, what I think I would find there. The figure is not a portrait. The vessels are not still life. They are emblems of a place that exists in my imagination as a complete world.

One day I plan to visit Morocco. I do not know what that will do to this painting. It may make it false. It may make it more true. That question stays open, and I am not in a hurry to resolve it.
