# Villefranche sur Mer

This painting is a reconstruction. Not of a place — I never finished seeing the place. I was in Nice, partway through a trip along the coast, when I got the call that my father had died. I flew back. The trip broke in half. What remained was not a complete memory but a fragment — the room, the shutters, the view of the sea I was about to walk toward. Villefranche stayed unfinished in my body the way an interrupted sentence stays in the ear. This painting is what I did with that interruption.

I started with Matisse's open window paintings, which I had been looking at in Nice, in the place where he actually made them. There is an absurd cruelty in that timing — standing in front of Matisse's rooms full of Mediterranean light, feeling the physical truth of those compositions, and then having that experience severed by a phone call from twelve thousand kilometres away. I didn't go back to finish the trip. Instead, years later, I rebuilt the room on a 24×24 panel, using Matisse's structure as a scaffold but filling it with my own materials. Papers from India. Travel notes. Fragments of text. The painting became a place I could enter that I never properly entered in life.

The composition is deliberately theatrical. Shutters and curtains frame the central opening the way a proscenium frames a stage. The sea is there — turquoise, distant, indifferent — but the painting keeps insisting on the room. On the enclosure. The coral and ochre walls press inward. The floor tilts forward. A small chair and table sit near the opening, holding the space the way a figure would hold it, though no figure sits there. There is a small face motif — almost a mask — embedded in the surface. I cannot fully explain why it is there. It appeared during the making and I left it. It watches.

The colour was the discovery. The reds and plums of the interior against the blues of the exterior created the threshold I was trying to paint — that boundary between the private room and the world outside. The warm enclosure and the cool distance. Every Matisse open window does this, but mine is doing it with grief in the walls, not sunlight. The warmth is not comfort. It is the heat of a room you cannot leave.

This painting sold, which means it lives somewhere else now. I have mixed feelings about that. It was a painting I needed to make to complete a trip that death interrupted. Whether it is finished as a painting, I am less certain. The chair is empty. The view is still there. I never did walk down to the water.
